A life lost during dialysis — and a call for accountability

On 27 December 2025, my father died during a dialysis session at Hearts International Hospital, Rawalpindi. This is a formal request for an independent investigation into the monitoring, staffing, emergency response, and handling of evidence surrounding his death.

The incident

What happened

My father was admitted on 26 December 2025 with critically high creatinine (7) and potassium (7), and we were assured of urgent dialysis. The nephrologist arrived on 27 December 2025, but dialysis was delayed until approximately 13:00.

At the start of dialysis, the nephrologist was present only briefly — for about one minute — reviewed the patient file, and left. The dialysis technician initiated the machine and left within approximately two minutes. The patient then remained on active dialysis without continuous qualified monitoring.

During this unattended period, visible significant bleeding occurred but was not immediately recognised. Medical staff were present nearby but were not engaged in active patient monitoring. The patient suddenly collapsed and became unresponsive. Hypovolemic shock was recognised only at a late stage. An emergency response was initiated, but it lacked dialysis-specific expertise — neither the dialysis technician nor the nephrologist was present at the time. The technician did not return, and the nephrologist arrived approximately 20–25 minutes after dialysis had started. The patient did not survive.

Concerns raised

Points requiring investigation

The following aspects of care appear to fall short of expected patient-safety standards and warrant independent review.

01

Delayed urgent dialysis

Despite life-threatening creatinine and potassium levels and assurances of urgency, dialysis was delayed until approximately 13:00.

02

No continuous monitoring

The patient remained on active dialysis without continuous qualified supervision after the nephrologist and technician left within minutes.

03

Unrecognised bleeding

Visible significant bleeding occurred during the unattended period and was not immediately recognised by the staff present.

04

Late recognition of shock

Hypovolemic shock was identified only at a late stage, after the patient had already collapsed and become unresponsive.

05

Inadequate emergency response

The response lacked dialysis-specific expertise; neither the technician nor the nephrologist was present when it was most needed.

06

Response & return delays

The technician did not return, and the nephrologist arrived approximately 20–25 minutes after dialysis started.

To the Punjab Healthcare Commission

Specific requests to the Commission

I request the Punjab Healthcare Commission to conduct an independent investigation and to secure and review the relevant evidence.

Conduct an independent investigation into dialysis monitoring and the standard of supervision provided during the session.
Examine staffing presence — whether qualified personnel remained with the patient throughout active dialysis.
Investigate the response delay and the adequacy of the emergency response to the patient's deterioration.
Assess adherence to safety protocols for dialysis from CVP connection through the full session.
Review the handling of CCTV evidence, including the hospital's refusal to provide a copy.
Secure and review the complete CCTV footage from the time of CVP connection through the entire dialysis session, and take appropriate regulatory action.
⚠ Evidence concern · CCTV

The footage exists — but a copy was refused

I requested the CCTV footage in writing. The hospital acknowledged its existence and showed me the footage, but later refused to provide a copy, citing “hospital policy” — without providing any written refusal or a copy of the policy.

A written application was submitted to the COO (In-charge of the Healthcare Establishment). To date, no written response and no policy document have been provided.

Seeking a fair, independent review

This site documents a patient-safety complaint in the words of the complainant. It seeks accountability, the preservation of evidence, and improvements that protect future patients.
